## 1. Motivation and Background

### 1.1 Unsolved Problems

Modern physics contains several deep unsolved problems.

The cosmological-constant problem: the vacuum energy density predicted by quantum field theory differs from the observed cosmological constant $\Lambda$ by a factor of $10^{120}$. This is the largest known discrepancy between theory and observation in the history of physics.

The dimension problem: existing theories cannot explain why space is three-dimensional. String theory postulates 10 or 11 dimensions, but why those numbers is an input brought in from outside the theory.

The physical-constants problem: dimensionless physical constants such as the fine-structure constant $\alpha \approx 1/137$ cannot be derived by any existing theory; they can only be determined by experiment.

### 1.2 Approach of the Present Theory

The present theory addresses these problems by deriving everything from a single axiom, minimising external parameter inputs and deriving physical quantities as mathematical necessities.

## 2. Axiom and Basic Setup

### 2.1 The Sole Axiom

Axiom Ω (self-generating axiom of existence)

$$\neg\exists \;\rightarrow\; \exists$$

The state of nothingness contains a self-referential contradiction: for the proposition "nothing exists" to hold, that proposition must itself exist. The recoil from this contradiction produces the minimal existence — one-dimensional energy.

Axiom Ω is an asymmetric transformation ($\neg\exists$ and $\exists$ are not equivalent) and carries odd parity. This property plays an essential role in the later acoustic-mode selection.

Ω non-degeneracy: the involution $\sigma$ of Axiom Ω (the exchange of $\neg\exists$ and $\exists$) is non-degenerate — that is, the action induced by $\sigma$ on the dual pairs generated by the theory is non-trivial. Here a dual pair refers to the 2-element set formed by the real-axis section and the imaginary-axis section of a single complexified structure within the theory; examples are the state pair $\{\neg\exists, \exists\}$ and the pair of the real axis (statistical face) and imaginary axis (quantum face) of the Mellin spectral plane. This clause reads Ω not merely as an exchange of two states but as the declaration of a non-degenerate duality. Under a weaker clause, counter-examples to the exponent identification survive; under the present clause, Theorem 6.9.2 holds unconditionally. Note that the decomposition of $\sigma$ into its even/odd parity eigenspaces is an eigenstructure of $\sigma$ itself and not a dual pair; it is outside the scope of this clause.

### 2.2 Minimal Unit of Existence

The minimal existence born from Axiom Ω is defined as one-dimensional ($n = 1$) energy. When energy can no longer be sustained, a critical point is reached and the next dimension is generated; dimensions increase one by one. The energy density at each dimension $n$ is written $\varepsilon(n)$.

## 3. Proof of Uniqueness of the Energy-Density Function

### 3.1 Postulates

The following two conditions are imposed.

Postulate B1 (closure): the first dimension is self-contained.

$$\varepsilon(1) \otimes 1 = \varepsilon(1)$$

Postulate B2 (scale invariance): only the ratio of dimensions determines the ratio of energies.

$$\frac{\varepsilon(an)}{\varepsilon(n)} = f(a) \quad \text{(independent of $n$)}$$

### 3.2 Derivation of the Unique Solution

Cauchy's functional equation $f(ab) = f(a)f(b)$ for continuous functions satisfying Postulate B2 has only the power-law as its solution.

An exponential $e^{-\beta n}$ gives $f(a) = e^{-\beta(a-1)n}$, which depends on $n$, so it is excluded. A logarithm is not scale-invariant and is likewise excluded.

The unique solution for the energy-density function is therefore

$$\boxed{\varepsilon(n) = E_{0} \cdot n^{-\alpha}}$$

where $\alpha > 0$ is the exponent expressing the dilution of energy as dimension increases, and $E_{0}$ is the Planck-scale energy.
